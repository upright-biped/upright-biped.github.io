Do Colors Have Opposites?

In kindergarten art class, they show us a color wheel like this. They say that the colors that will contrast each other the strongest are across from each other, opposites:

  • red and green
  • orange and blue
  • yellow and purple
But later on in physics class, we learn that color is different frequencies of light. Colors progress down the spectrum linearly, so how can they have opposites?

How We Detect Color

The color sensing cones in our eyes come in 3 sensitivities, each specializing in detecting a different range of light frequencies. The peak sensitivities of these are red, green, and blue.

When we see 2 frequencies of light together, our brain processes them as the average of the 2. Pixels on screens take advantage of this. Instead of changing the frequency of light, there are 3 lights which each change their intensity. So while there is no single frequency of purple light, our minds assign mixed blue and red light a color, like it assigns a color to any other frequency or combination of frequencies. From these you can see that again the colors have been arranged circularly again, but are the ones across from other "opposite?"

Additive Color

White light is how our brains interpret the average of all 3 frequencies together. When we overlap the colors like a venn diagram, you can see that the combinations equal a lack of the color across from them. This is most clear in the pixel image, where in the spots 2 colors overlap, you can see the missing space for the 3rd color.

  • cyan is all light but red
  • magenta is all light but green
  • yellow is all light but blue
In that way, these colors are opposites! Compare that to the standard color wheel!

Light vs Pigment

So lets assume that the primary colors we use for light correspond to the colors each cone detects, and that is why they are primary. When objects are reflecting light and not generating it, the color they appear is the frequencies it does not absorb. This means the primary colors are inverse to light. If an object appears yellow( red and green) under white ( red green blue) light, then if you shine it with cyan ( green and blue) light, it absorbs blue light to only reflect green. This means that objects or pigments, which have a color in white light, will in some color of light reflect no color, appearing black.
